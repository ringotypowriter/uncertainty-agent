## Abstract

Thaumatin is a low-calorie protein sweetener extracted from *Thaumatococcus daniellii* Benth. Its ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake) was concluded as "not specified" by the EFSA (European Food Safety Authority). This study was conducted to select a sample preparation method and validate the proposed method by applying a matrix-matched calibration curve using HPLC-UVD. In the results, the calibration curve (5‚Äì200 mg/L) represented good linearity ($r^2 \geq 0.9995$). LOD and LOQ ranged from 1.28 to 2.00 mg/L and from 3.89 to 6.07 mg/L, respectively. The intra- and inter-day accuracies and precisions ranged from 91.5 to 103.0%RSD and from 0.50 to 3.42%RSD, respectively. Furthermore, the relative expanded uncertainty was evaluated and the result satisfied the CODEX guideline ($\leq 16$%). Additionally, the application of the validated method was confirmed by using thaumatin-containing foods distributed in Korea. Thaumatin intake and exposure assessments for consumers can be conducted based on this study.

## Introduction

Sweeteners are used to impart food with a sweet taste and can be classified into natural sweeteners and synthetic sweeteners. Natural sweeteners can be further classified into saccharide sweeteners (e.g., glucose, sucrose, fructose, and honey) and non-saccharide sweeteners (e.g., terpenoids, proteins, and polyols) (Priya et al., 2011). Sucrose is a disaccharide composed of fructose and glucose. It is made by extracting sugar juice from sugar cane or sugar beet and removing impurities. Excessive intake of fructose can cause a myriad of health problems. Unlike glucose, fructose does not stimulate the secretion of insulin, which controls blood sugar, and leptin, which is a satiety hormone that prevents overeating (Elliott et al., 2002). Saccharide sweeteners are caloric sugars that provide energy in the form of hydrocarbons.

As consumer awareness of health problems, such as type 2 diabetes, obesity, and tooth decay, caused by overeating sugar/sweetener-laden food has increased, so too has the demand for non-caloric sweeteners (Joseph et al., 2019). Non-sweeteners or low-caloric sweeteners are sugar substitutes that can be divided into high-intensity sweeteners and low-intensity sweeteners according to their sweetness (Yun and Kim, 2022). They can also be divided into synthetic (e.g., aspartame, saccharin, and acesulfame potassium), semi-synthetic (e.g., neohesperidine dihydrochalcone), and natural sweeteners (e.g., steviol glycoside and thaumatin) (Shah and de Jager, 2017). Thaumatin, monellin, mabinline, pentadin, brazzein, curculin, and miraculin are among various sweet proteins that can be found naturally in tropical plants. These proteins have unique binding sites, resulting in different perceptions of sweetness, which range in intensity

Thaumatin is extracted from the fruit of the African plant *Thaumatococcus daniellii* Benth (Katemfe fruit) (Kelada et al., 2021). It is composed of 207 amino acid residues with intramolecular disulfide bonds that can be classified as a low-calorie protein sweetener. Commercial thaumatin, extracted from the fruit, is a light brown powder with high solubility in water at room temperature and exists in five isoforms (thaumatin I, II, III, a, and b). Among them, thaumatin I and II are the main forms and consist of three domains (De Vos et al., 1985; Temussi, 2006). The third domain is known to contain critical residues that contribute to the sweet taste of thaumatin (Kaneko and Kitabatake, 2001; Masuda et al., 2022). As a sweet protein, the molecular weight of thaumatin I and II are both over 22,000, and they are 2000 times sweeter than sugar. This intense sweetness develops gradually (Agboola et al., 2014) and because thaumatin has the unique ability to mask bitter and astringent tastes, it can also be used in medicine (Fry, 2012). Moreover, the glycemic index of thaumatin is close to 0, meaning that it does not cause blood sugar spikes and is thus suitable for patients with diabetes (Onuawuchi et al., 2022).

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has assigned Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) status to thaumatin I and II, as well as to recombinant versions of the proteins, for use as a flavor modifier (Kelada et al., 2021). In Europe, thaumatin is classified by the European Food and Safety Authority (EFSA) as E 957 and approved for use as a sweetener and taste enhancer (Kelada et al., 2021). In addition, the EFSA conducted a re-evaluation of thaumatin (E 957) in 2021; the maximum acceptable standard for all animal species was 1‚Äì5 mg/kg, and it was concluded that it was not dangerous to consumers (EFSA, 2021). Subsequently, the acceptable daily intake (ADI) of thaumatin I and II was classified as ‚Äúnot specified‚Äù because the proteins passed the safety evaluation for allergy, digestibility, and toxicity (Kassara et al., 2022). Moreover, the analysis of food additive levels in food products and assessments of dietary intake have become increasingly important in recent years (Park et al., 2024; Seo et al., 2024).

Regarding its physicochemical properties, thaumatin is more stable under acidic conditions than neutral and alkaline conditions, and its sweet taste is greatly affected by pH (Kaneko and Kitabatake, 2001). Although it is a natural sweetener and attractive sugar substitute, few studies related to the analysis of thaumatin used as a sweetener in food have been conducted. This study focused on selecting an appropriate syringe filter for the filtration of large protein molecules and developing a preparation method based on the physicochemical properties of thaumatin. Using the proposed method, an analytical method for quantification of thaumatin using matrix-matched calibration was validated using HPLC-UVD, and the measurement uncertainty was evaluated. The applicability of the developed method was demonstrated by quantifying the thaumatin content of various foods collected from a local market in the Republic of Korea.

## Materials and methods

### Materials and reagents

The thaumatin standard used in this study was a product of TCI (Tokyo, Japan). Trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich (Saint Louis, MO, USA), HPLC grade acetonitrile and HPLC grade water were used for the preparation of the mobile phase from JT Baker Chemical Co. (Radnor, PA, USA). To monitor the use of thaumatin in the Korean food industry, samples were selected through market research from processed food products explicitly labeled with thaumatin as an ingredient. This targeted selection ensured the relevance of the analysis, focusing on products where thaumatin is intentionally utilized in food formulations. Among them, a total of 18 food products, including *soju* (a well-known Korean alcoholic beverage), beverages, and candy were selected as monitoring items from the Korean food market.

### Analytical instrument

For the analysis of thaumatin, the applied method was developed with slight modifications based on references from the previous studies (Lukiƒá and Horvat, 2020; Marangon et al., 2009; Van Sluyter et al., 2009). An UltiMate 3000 HPLC system (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) equipped with a Cosmosil Protein-R column ($4.6 \times 150$ mm, 5 $\mu$m) was used. The detection wavelength and the column temperature were set to 277 nm and 30 ¬∞C, respectively. The mobile phases were 0.05% TFA in 20% acetonitrile (A) and 0.05% TFA in 60% acetonitrile (B). The gradient conditions were 0‚Äì20 min, 100‚Äì0% A. The flow rate and injection volume were 1.0 mL/min and 20 $\mu$L, respectively.

### Standard solutions preparation

Standard solutions were prepared by precisely weighing out 100 mg of thaumatin into 100 mL volumetric flasks with water being added to reach the same volume. The standard solutions were prepared at concentrations of 5‚Äì200 mg/L by diluting the respective standard stock solution with water.

## Optimization of filter selection and extraction temperature

A suitable syringe filter was selected by a comparison of the following four types using a 5 mL Kovax-Syringe (Korea Vaccine Co., Ltd., Jongno-gu, Korea): 13 mm-diameter, 0.45 ¬µm pore size polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE; Millipore, Burlington, USA), 13 mm-diameter, 0.45 ¬µm pore size polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF; Millipore), 13 mm diameter, 0.45 ¬µm pore size polyethersulfone (PES; Millipore), and 15 mm-diameter, 0.45 ¬µm pore size regenerated cellulose (RC; Sartorius, G√∂ttingen, Germany). After filtering thaumatin standard solutions (10 and 50 mg/kg) three times each, with four different syringes, the average relative recovery was compared. To select the appropriate temperature for sample preparation and to assess stability at different temperatures, thaumatin standard solutions (10 and 50 mg/kg) were heated at temperatures ranging from 25 to 100 ¬∞C for 0 to 180 min, with intervals of 15 ¬∞C and 20 min, respectively.

## Sample preparation

*Soju*, beverages, and candy, which were the main food matrices containing thaumatin, were used for method validation. Sample preparation differed depending on the type of sample (*soju*, liquid, solid, or powder). An EYELA N-1300 rotary evaporator (Tokyo, Japan) equipped with a vacuum pump (EYELA NVP-1000) was set at 45 ¬∞C to evaporate 100 mL *soju* to 10‚Äì15 mL. Evaporation was continued using a nitrogen evaporator (ANPEL, Shanghai, China) at 50 ¬∞C until the volume of the sample reached 2 mL. One gram of liquid sample was diluted 2 times with water, solid and powder samples were diluted 5 times also with water, and the mixture was then sonicated for 15 min using a Power sonic 420 ultrasonic water bath (Hwashin tech, Seoul, South Korea). After cooling at room temperature, the sample was filtered through a 0.45 ¬µm pores size PTFE syringe filter.

## Method validation

According to the FDA, the Official Methods of Analysis of the Association of Official Analytical Chemists (AOAC) guideline Q2 (R1), and CODEX (FDA, 2019; ICH, 2005; Thompson et al., 2002), specificity, linearity, limit of detection (LOD), limit of quantification (LOQ), accuracy, and precision were evaluated. The method validation was conducted using *soju*, beverage, and candy as matrix-matched standards. By comparing the blank, thaumatin standard solution, and sample, specificity was confirmed, as there were no interfering substances. Furthermore, to determine linearity, thaumatin content in each matrix was analyzed in the range of 5‚Äì200 mg/L with seven replicates. To calculate LOD and LOQ, the SD of the y-axis intercept was divided by the average of the slope using the three lowest concentrations of the calibration curve, and then the resultant value was multiplied by 3.3 and 10, respectively.

The matrix effect (ME) denotes the differential response exhibited by the analyte when present in a matrix as opposed to its presence in a solvent. This variation can be quantified through the assessment of either the peak area or the slope pertaining to the analyte in both the matrix and the solvent.

$$\text{ME } (\%) = \left( \frac{\text{peak area or slope}_{\text{matrix}}}{\text{peak area or slope}_{\text{solvent}}} - 1 \right) \times 100$$

If ME is 0%, it means there is no effect from the matrix, and if it exceeds ¬± 20%, it is considered significant (Sante, 2021). Accuracy is the similarity of the analysis result to the true value. By spiking 20, 50, and 100 mg/kg of standard solutions in each matrix, accuracy was calculated as a recovery rate (%). Relative standard deviation (%RSD) was evaluated using the spiked matrix for intra-day precision, which was repeated six times per day, and inter-day precision, which was repeated three times for 3 days under the same conditions. In addition, the Horwitz ratio (HorRat value) was calculated. To this end, the RSD$_R$, which is the reproducibility (%) that can be calculated from the experimental result value, was divided by the PRSD$_R$, which is the RSD value predicted from the Horwitz equation (Horwitz and Richard, 2006).

## Measurement uncertainty evaluation

Measurement uncertainty is a parameter of values that can reasonably be attributed to a measured value. To estimate the uncertainty, the mathematical and statistical methods suggested by EURACHEM (Ellison and Williams, 2012) were used in this study. The measurement uncertainty was estimated for the standard stock solution preparation (uSSS), sample preparation (uSP), calibration curve (uCal.), and repeated measurement of samples (uRP). Moreover, the relative expanded uncertainty ($U_c$) was evaluated using the coverage factor ($k$) of 2 at the 95% confidence level.

## Statistical analysis

To optimize the development of the sample preparation method, the statistical significance was determined based on a comparison of the relative recovery values according to the filter type used and heat treatment temperature of the thaumatin standard solution. Analysis of variance (ANOVA), which was used to compare the variances within and between groups and the average, was also used to verify that the differences between the data were statistically significant. Results were represented as the average and standard deviation (SD), and SAS software (Version 9.4; SAS

## Results and discussion

### Sample preparation

#### Filter comparison for recovery

PTFE, PVDF, RC, and PES filters are widely recognized for their low protein-binding properties, making them suitable candidates for protein filtration. To identify the optimal filter for thaumatin, we performed a comparative analysis of these filters, evaluating their binding affinity to thaumatin protein and recovery rates across varying filtering amount volumes (1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 mL) depending on concentration. Comparing the different types of filters, the relative recoveries of thaumatin solutions at 10 and 50 mg/L were 79.5‚Äì101.9% and 98.8‚Äì100.5% (PTFE syringe filter), 0‚Äì39.5% and 22.0‚Äì83.8% (PVDF syringe filter), 0‚Äì26.2% and 48.6‚Äì95.6% (RC syringe filter), and 4.9‚Äì62.0% and 80.2‚Äì97.7% (PES syringe filter), respectively. The results demonstrated that the PTFE filter exhibited the lowest binding affinity to thaumatin while achieving the highest recovery efficiency. Consequently, the PTFE filter was selected and applied to the sample pretreatment process. PTFE is a commonly used membrane in syringe filters, valued for its superior hydrophobicity, chemical stability, high heat resistance, and low protein binding (Akduman, 2023). Additionally, PTFE filters have been employed in a previous study on thaumatin analysis, demonstrating a consistent methodology with that used in the current research (Lukiƒá and Horvat, 2020). ## Application

To confirm the applicability of the proposed method using HPLC, 18 foods distributed in Korea, including *soju*, fruit/vegetable beverages, and candy, were collected. The results were expressed as the average and SD calculated by repeating the analysis three times were N.D.‚Äì0.14 mg/kg of thaumatin in *soju*, 2.81‚Äì3.95 mg/kg in fruit/vegetable beverage, and N.D.‚Äì28.54 mg/kg in candy (Table 3). Additionally, the HPLC chromatograms of the soju and candy samples are shown in Supplementary Fig. 2.

Thaumatin can be added to various foods (Kaneko and Kitabatake, 2001), including those used in this study. According to the EFSA (2021), in non-alcoholic beverages, the maximum permissible limit of thaumatin as a flavor enhancer is 0.5 mg/kg, whereas that permitted in flavored fermented milk products and syrup-form food supplements is 5 and 400 mg/kg, respectively, Furthermore, in ice-creams and sweets, thaumatin is permitted for use at concentrations up to 50 mg/kg (Mortensen, 2006). If this background is considered, the thaumatin contents determined in foods in this study are acceptable, and the developed method can be proposed as an appropriate quantifying method for detecting thaumatin in various processed foods.

In conclusion, several food groups in which thaumatin can be used were investigated. In order to develop the sample preparation method suitable for various food matrices, a PTFE filter was selected as the most suitable from a set of four filters by confirming that the relative recovery may be affected by the type of filter and filtration volume. Additionally, thaumatin in *soju* was concentrated under temperature conditions of 45‚Äì50 ¬∞C because decreased stability occurred when the temperature was exceeded 70 ¬∞C. This study is differentiated from previous studies in that it developed an analytical method for detecting thaumatin using HPLC-UVD and reviewed the reliability of the proposed method through the method validation and its application. Furthermore, based on this study, the proposed method can be used for intake and exposure assessments for consumers by analyzing the concentration of thaumatin in foods distributed in Korea.
